What is the probability a random answer to this question will be correct?
Sunday, January 28th, 2018

Maybe you've seen this problem or its variations before:

By considering each possibility in turn, we can show that there is no answer.

  • Suppose that A were correct. It agrees with D. The probability of selecting either A or D by random chance is 50%, since there are four answers. So if A were correct, the probability of answering correctly by choosing at random would be 50% -- but then A would not be correct. Hence A is not the answer.
  • Suppose that B were correct. No other answer agrees with it, so the probability of being correct by chance would be the probability of selecting B by chance; that is, 25%. But then B would not be correct. Hence B is not the answer.
  • C) cannot be correct by the same argument that applies to B.
  • D cannot be correct by the same argument that applies to A.

You may notice that there are fairly heavy restrictions on which problems of this kind have answers. It seems like we should be able to classify them all.

One observation: in order to be correct an answer must, at the very least, give a probability of the form $(\frac{k}{n})$% where n is the number of other answers -- if we have four answers and one of them is 37%, we can throw it right out since there is just no way to achieve that probability.

Another observation: if an answer $\frac{k}{n}$ is not repeated enough to constitute exactly $\frac{k}{n}$ of the n answers, it cannot be correct. (This was what we used to analyze the original problem.)
